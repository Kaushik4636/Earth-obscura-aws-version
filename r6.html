Bio-Geographic Isolation

The Southern Sanctuary.

Locked between the Andes Mountains and the Pacific Ocean, the Valdivian forest is a "biogeographic island"—a temperate rainforest that has evolved in total isolation for millions of years.

Unlike the steaming jungles of the equator, the Valdivian is a **temperate rainforest**. It is characterized by cool summers and wet, mild winters. This isolation has resulted in a high degree of endemism; over 90% of the seed plants found here exist nowhere else on the planet.

The Alerce: Sentinels of Time

The crown jewel of the Valdivian is the Fitzroya cupressoides, or Alerce tree. These conifers are among the oldest living things on Earth, with some individuals verified to be over 3,600 years old. Their slow growth rate is an adaptation to the nutrient-poor, waterlogged soils of the Chilean fjords, allowing them to outlast nearly every other tree species in the southern hemisphere.

Glacial Refugia

During the last Ice Age, while much of the world's northern forests were wiped out by advancing glaciers, portions of the Valdivian coast remained ice-free. These areas served as refugia—biological lifeboats where species survived and later recolonized the continent. This deep history is why the forest retains a "primitive" feel, dominated by ancient ferns and bamboo understories.
