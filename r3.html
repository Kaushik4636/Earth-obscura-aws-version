Terrestrial Carbon Sequestration

The Heart of Darkness.

Spanning across six African nations, the Congo Basin is a massive green monolith that stores more carbon in its peatlands than all the trees in the forest combined.

The Congo Basin is characterized by its high humidity and relatively low elevation. Unlike the Amazon, which is largely terrestrial, much of the Congo is a mosaic of rivers, swamps, and flooded forests. This environment has created the **Cuvette Centrale**—the largest complex of tropical peatlands in the world.

The Carbon Vault

The peatlands of the Congo are an essential component of the Earth's climate regulation. Peat forms when organic matter—leaves and wood—falls into waterlogged soil where the lack of oxygen prevents decomposition. This "locks" the carbon into the ground. If these peatlands were to dry out or be disturbed, they would release a massive "carbon bomb" into the atmosphere.

A River of Giants

The Congo River is the second largest in the world by discharge and the deepest river ever recorded, reaching depths of over 220 meters. This depth creates unique evolutionary barriers; species on one side of the river are often entirely different from those on the other. This phenomenon, known as **riverine barrier hypothesis**, is a primary driver of the basin's incredible biodiversity.
