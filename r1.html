Tropical Hydrology

The Living Atmosphere.

The Amazon is not just a forest; it is a biological machine of such immense scale that it creates its own weather, recycling moisture across a continent.

Covering over 5.5 million square kilometers, the Amazon Basin contains half of the planet's remaining tropical rainforests. It is driven by **evapotranspiration**, where trillions of trees pump water from the soil into the atmosphere, creating "flying rivers" that carry more water than the Amazon River itself.

Stratification of Life

Survival in the rainforest is determined by light. The forest is divided into four distinct layers: the **Emergent layer** (the giants), the **Canopy** (the biological roof), the **Understory** (low light), and the **Forest Floor** (perpetual shadow). Each layer houses unique species that have adapted to specific levels of solar radiation and humidity.

The Nutrient Cycle

Paradoxically, rainforest soil is often nutrient-poor. Because of the high rainfall, nutrients are quickly leached away. Life depends on a rapid decomposition cycle—fungi and bacteria break down fallen matter so quickly that nutrients are reabsorbed by the trees almost instantly, bypassing the soil entirely.
